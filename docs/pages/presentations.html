> Modeling interactions between sewage systems and green spaces in Chicago using a dual-drainage model - case study Indian Boundary Prairies

[ AGU 2024 @ Washington, DC ]

🪧 Go to poster 🪧

Large-scale hydrodynamical models (km) couple surface and groundwater interactions but do not include drainage infrastructure. Detailed hydraulic models (m) capture sewers and street flow but are limited to design purposes and do not capture groundwater interactions, which are key when evaluating the role of green spaces in flooding mitigation in urban areas. How can we build a model that captures surface, groundwater and sewer flows?

 

> Hydraulic and biochemical optimization of soil-aquifer systems impacted by bioclogging

[ AGU 2023 @ San Francisco, CA ]

🛝 Go to presentation 🛝

[Read abstract] Bioclogging severly hinders Soil-Aquifer Treament (SAT) performance as microbial growth supported by the nutrient-rich wastewater fills the pore space. Clogging is reversed operationally with drying periods to interrupt water and nutrient delivery, replenish oxygen, and desiccate biofilms. However, the total volume of water that ends up being treated is severely diminished because of those drying periods. We built a 3D finite-volume model in OpenFOAM that couples SAT infiltration, unsaturated flow, reactive transport, and microbial growth, with changes in the hydraulic conductivity field due to bioclogging.

 

> Turbulence-driven hyporheic flow: connecting fine particle deposition, clogging, and nutrient cycles

[ EES Seminar Fall/2023 @ Northwestern Univ, Evanston, IL
Séminaire METIS @ Sorbonne Université, Paris, France ]

🛝 Go to presentation 🛝

Turbulence accounts for a significant fraction of fine particle delivery to the hyporheic zone. Turbulent transport of fine particles contributes substantially to interfacial clogging that attenuates hyporheic exchange. A numerical model is presented that integrates interfacial turbulence, HE of suspended particles and bed clogging.

> Modeling unsaturated flow and bioclogging to optimize SAT operation strategies

[ AGU 2022 @ Chicago, IL ]

🛝 Go to presentation 🛝

[Read abstract] Soil-Aquifer Treatment (SAT) systems are useful as a polishing step for municipal wastewater reclamation. Ideally, SAT systems operate with both rapid water infiltration and high nutrient transformation rates. But processes cannot be maximized simultaneously, however, as microbial growth reduces soil permeability and decreases infiltration rates. We hypothesize that the optimal D/F ratio increases with the total time of SAT operation as recalcitrant metabolic products accumulate in the soil. To test this hypothesis, column experiments are modeled under different D/F ratios, and infiltration, reaction, and clogging rates are calculated as a function of time for each experiment.

 

> Turbulence drives hyporheic exchange and fine particle deposition across the entire riverbed surface

[ AGU 2021 @ New Orleans, IL ]

🪧 Go to poster 🪧

[Read abstract] Fine particles (FP) in rivers play a key role in controlling rates of hyporheic exchange. FP are filtered within the riverbed, which leads to pore clogging that decreases the sediment permeability locally and redistributes hyporheic flow. Field and laboratory observations have shown that FP deposit everywhere on the riverbed, but the mechanisms responsible for the observed deposition are not entirely clear because currently available models do not fully resolve all relevant scales of coupling between hyporheic flow, particle filtration and riverbed clogging. We hypothesized that particle deposition in these regions results from turbulence in and around the sediment-water interface, which has not been fully resolved in prior models.

 

> Fine particle filtration in riverbeds and its impact in hyporheic exchanges

[ AGU 2020 @ Remotely because of COVID-19 ]

🪧 Go to poster 🪧

[Read abstract] It has been shown that FP low concentrations in the surface water are an important driver in clogging pores in the bedforms and in hindering hyporheic exchanges. Hence, we have built a numerical model able to represent the spatial distribution of FP accumulation in riverbeds, thus, able to assess the spatial variability of permeability and the decrease of hyporheic exchange that this entails. Although the proposed model is a non-linear system where both flow and fine particle deposition depend on each other's evolution, a steady state can be reached when the pores in the shallowest layers are completely clogged. At this point, transport to deeper regions is completely hindered and the connection between surface water and groundwater is basically lost.
